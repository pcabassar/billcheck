# V0.1 cases — Segment 3: Commercial, out-of-network / surprise

> **Status: INPUT (V0.1).** 5 real, cited cases. Template v2.1. Index: [../v0.1-cases.md](../v0.1-cases.md). _2026-06-17._

### 3.1 — NICU twins, ~$80K (NSA "emergency" loophole)
- **Insurance:** Commercial Cigna (employer; self-funded vs fully-insured undocumented). Hospital fully **OON** for the newborns; family expected to hit a $10K OON OOP max. **Engine-relevant? No** (turns on NSA emergency classification).
- **Money:** billed **$117,084**; family owed **~$80,000**; → collections.
- **What happened:** Cigna refused to treat the NICU admission as an "emergency" because the surrogate entered via obstetrics (not the ER) → NSA emergency protection sidestepped. Family hired **Resolve Medical Bills** (a competitor advocate); final $ outcome not documented.
- **Levers:** NSA emergency arg via **prudent-layperson/EMTALA** standard + CMS complaint; internal appeal; external review; charity care/negotiation. **Agent must ask:** self-funded vs fully-insured? does the record document emergent delivery/fetal distress?
- **Signal:** insurers dodge NSA emergency protection by **reclassifying the admission route.**
- **Source:** [KFF, Feb 23 2022](https://kffhealthnews.org/news/article/nicu-surprise-bill-loophole-no-surprises-act/)

### 3.2 — OON neonatologists at an in-network hospital, $12,531 → $289 (state law won)
- **Insurance:** Commercial **fully-insured** BCBS-IL (employer); facility **in-network** (Prentice/Northwestern), but the NICU **physicians (Lurie Children's, separate entity) were OON.** **Engine-relevant? No.**
- **Money:** billed **$14,624.55**; patient asked to pay **$12,531.58**; after resolution owed **$289.63** (coinsurance).
- **What happened:** ~3-year fight; both BCBS and Lurie reps **denied knowledge of the IL surprise-billing law (§356z.3a)**; the **employer's insurance broker** finally pressured BCBS to pay at in-network rates. (NSA now covers this nationally.)
- **Levers:** NSA §2799B-2 (OON ancillary at in-network facility) + **state law (fully-insured only)**; internal appeal; **employer HR/broker intervention** (the decisive lever here); state insurance-commissioner complaint.
- **Signal:** the **employer benefits broker** is a real, under-used lever; payers/providers ignore clear law until an intermediary pushes; **fully-insured vs self-funded** decides whether state law applies.
- **Source:** [KFF, Jan 30 2023](https://kffhealthnews.org/news/article/a-baby-spent-36-days-in-an-in-network-nicu-why-did-the-hospital-next-door-send-a-bill/)

### 3.3 — Routine prenatal labs at a hospital lab, $9,520 billed / $2,390 owed (facility-fee trap; NSA doesn't apply)
- **Insurance:** Commercial Anthem BCBS; provider **in-network** (Texas Health hospital lab). **No OON/NSA issue.** **Engine-relevant? Maybe** — possible **ACA-preventive-services** miscoding (HIV/HepB/syphilis screening in pregnancy are $0-cost-share preventive services).
- **Money:** billed **$9,520.02**; allowed **$6,700.50**; insurer paid **$4,310.38**; patient owed **$2,390.12**.
- **What happened:** lawful in-network hospital-lab pricing (facility fees >> independent lab). Outcome post-publication not documented.
- **Levers:** check **ACA preventive-services $0 rule** (likely the strongest angle — were these billed as diagnostic vs preventive, and did the plan wrongly apply the deductible?); negotiate/charity care (nonprofit hospital); route future labs to an independent lab.
- **Signal:** "in-network" ≠ "fair" — **site-of-service/facility-fee** + **preventive-services miscoding** are billing-error angles even with no surprise bill.
- **Source:** [KFF, Nov 20 2023](https://kffhealthnews.org/health-industry/routine-bloodwork-lab-work-tests-surprise-bill/)

### 3.4 — Infant air ambulance, $97,599, insurer pays $0 on "medical necessity" (NSA covered, but denial end-run)
- **Insurance:** Commercial Cigna (employer); air ambulance **OON**. NSA covers air ambulance vs *balance billing* — but Cigna denied the **whole claim as "not medically necessary,"** which the NSA does **not** override. **Engine-relevant? No.**
- **Money:** billed **$97,599**; insurer paid **$0**; family owed **$97,599** (in dispute).
- **What happened:** 2 internal appeals denied; the origin hospital **declined to write a support letter**; family drafting its own external-review appeal. After KFF contacted Cigna, a rep offered to help with the final appeal. Unresolved at publication.
- **Levers:** **external independent medical review** (the decisive lever) + **physician attestation** that ground transport was contraindicated; NSA cost-share cap *if* coverage is established; CA DMHC (fully-insured) vs DOL (self-funded) complaint.
- **Signal:** post-NSA, insurers shifted from balance-billing to **medical-necessity denials** — a coverage denial the NSA can't fix; getting **provider clinical support** is often the hard blocker.
- **Source:** [KFF/NPR, Mar 25 2024](https://kffhealthnews.org/news/article/medical-necessity-rsv-infant-air-ambulance-surprise-bill/)

### 3.5 — Mount Sinai telehealth, $660, consent form timestamped AFTER the visit (NSA violation, but stuck)
- **Insurance:** Commercial Empire BCBS (NY); system **in-network** but the specific telehealth physician **OON**; insurer paid **$0**. App had shown ~$60. **Engine-relevant? No.**
- **Money:** expected ~**$60**; billed **$660**; insurer paid **$0**; owed **$660** (unresolved, still receiving bills a year+ later).
- **What happened:** Mount Sinai cited a consent form, withheld it for ~a year, and it was **timestamped after the visit** — Mount Sinai **admitted this violates the NSA** notice-and-consent rule. Neither side filed **IDR** (the ~$400–800 arbitration cost is **uneconomical for a $660 bill**). Unresolved.
- **Levers:** **CMS No Surprises complaint** (provider admitted non-compliance; up to $10K/violation) + **NY DFS** surprise-billing complaint + NY law/credit-report ban; demand write-off. *Open Q:* is the telehealth platform a hospital outpatient dept (NSA applies) or not?
- **Signal:** the **NSA's IDR is economically broken for sub-$1,000 bills** → small surprise bills get stuck even when the violation is admitted; **regulator complaint > arbitration** for small claims.
- **Source:** [KFF/NPR, Dec 19 2023](https://kffhealthnews.org/news/article/telehealth-surprise-bill-december-bill-of-the-month/)

---
**Segment signals:** (1) NSA has **big exploitable loopholes** — emergency-by-admission-route, **medical-necessity denials** (coverage ≠ balance billing), post-visit consent, and **IDR-too-expensive-for-small-bills**. The agent must know these, not just "NSA protects you." (2) **Fully-insured vs self-funded** is a load-bearing question (decides state-law leverage). (3) New levers surfaced: **employer benefits broker**, **ACA preventive-services $0 rule**, **external medical review + provider attestation**. (4) "In-network" ≠ fair (facility-fee/site-of-service traps).
