### 5.1 — Medicaid: out-of-state heart attack → balance-billed $77,574 because the hospital wouldn't file with the home-state plan
- **Insurance:** Florida Medicaid (FFS); the only payer (no secondary). Monument Health (SD) is enrolled only in SD + bordering states, so it's OON for FL Medicaid and **never submitted a claim** — it billed the patient directly, which is **illegal** (42 USC §1396a(a)(25)(C); balance-billing a Medicaid patient for a covered emergency is barred). **Engine-relevant? No** (balance-billing prohibition).
- **Money:** billed to patient **$77,574.44**; Medicaid paid **$0** (no claim filed); patient legally owed **$0** (federal out-of-state-emergency Medicaid rule, 42 CFR §431.52).
- **What happened:** Wirt (62) had a heart attack out of state on Christmas; hospital staff offered only an unaffordable payment plan and **never mentioned charity care**. Nothing moved until **KFF contacted the hospital → balance dropped to $0 via charity care within days** (the underlying enroll-and-file issue was never actually fixed).
- **Levers:** written demand citing the **Medicaid balance-billing prohibition** (enroll + file, or zero it); complaint to the home-state Medicaid program/MCO; **request the 501(c)(3) charity-care application in writing**; state AHCA/insurance complaint; press (what actually worked).
- **Signal:** **Medicaid patients owe $0 for covered emergencies but get illegally balance-billed anyway**, especially **out-of-state** where the hospital isn't enrolled; staff hide charity care; **press resolved it, not the legal right**.
- **Source:** [KFF, May 29 2025](https://kffhealthnews.org/news/article/out-of-state-surprise-billing-medicaid-bill-of-the-month-may-2025/)

### 5.2 — Coordination of benefits: the "birthday rule" flipped primary/secondary on a NICU newborn → $270,951, 18 months of runaround
- **Insurance:** **two commercial employer plans** (no Medicaid). The **birthday rule** makes the parent whose birthday is earlier in the year primary → dad's CommunityCare-OK plan (high-deductible, OON-heavy) became primary; mom's Blue KC (which had been paying as primary) **clawed back** its payments and re-billed. Mom's plan was **self-funded/ERISA**, so the state DOI couldn't help. **Engine-relevant? No** (COB-order error).
- **Money:** total billed **$270,951** (NICU **$207,455**); after correct COB, primary paid $16,605 + secondary $19,116 and a **$7,504.51 balance was written off → patient owed $0** — but it took **18 months**.
- **What happened:** parents assumed mom's plan was primary; Blue KC discovered the second plan, applied the birthday rule retroactively, reversed payments → providers chased the family. The **Kansas DOI declined (ERISA preemption)**; ultimately mom's **HR/benefits officer intervened** and it resolved at $0. Prompted a proposed KS birthday-rule reform.
- **Levers:** **fix COB order proactively in writing with both insurers** (submit COB questionnaires before discharge); **ERISA appeal (180-day window) for a self-funded plan** vs **state-DOI complaint for a fully-insured plan**; escalate via **employer HR/benefits advocate** (decisive here); ask hospitals for an **"insurance dispute" billing hold**; dispute any collections in writing; press.
- **Signal:** **dual coverage can create chaos, not safety**; the **fully-insured-vs-self-funded (ERISA)** split decides who can help — *the same load-bearing question as the OON segment*; **employer HR/broker is a real, under-used lever**; multi-biller/multi-payer reconciliation is exactly what the activity-log + amounts model is for.
- **Source:** [KFF/NPR, Jan 27 2021](https://kffhealthnews.org/news/article/baby-blues-first-time-parents-blindsided-by-the-birthday-rule-and-a-207455-nicu-bill/)

### 5.3 — Dual-eligible (QMB): systemic illegal balance billing — NY AG restitution + CFPB/CMS enforcement
- **Insurance:** **QMB / dual-eligible** — Medicare pays first, Medicaid covers the cost-share, and the provider may collect **$0** from the patient (any copay/coinsurance/deductible billing is barred, 42 CFR §422.504(g)(1)(iii)). **Engine-relevant? No** (balance-billing prohibition). _Documented at the systemic level (no single named patient)._
- **Money:** NY AG v. **Mobile Life** (ground ambulance): restitution **+12%/yr interest** for everyone balance-billed since Jun 2016, **$100k fine**, collections zeroed, **credit reports corrected**. CFPB (2024): **~17%** of Medicare complaints reference QMB status; ~11% report collection of debts **not owed**; ~8.7M QMBs nationally.
- **What happened:** most QMBs **don't know they're protected and pay** (CMS 2015 study); enforcement is rare and patchwork — the NY AG action required an **independent auditor** to even find the victims; CFPB+CMS issued a **2024 joint statement** restating the rule but created **no individual refund mechanism**.
- **Levers:** written demand to provider/collector citing the **QMB billing prohibition + CMS MLN SE1128** (collector must cease; provider must refund); CMS / 1-800-Medicare complaint (CMS can sanction/exclude); **CFPB complaint** (FDCPA — debt not owed); state Medicaid + AG consumer-protection complaints; **credit-bureau disputes** (FCRA 30-day investigation). Justice in Aging has a model QMB letter.
- **Signal:** **QMB = a hard $0, but providers/collectors bill anyway and most patients pay**; the win is often **refund + credit repair**, not just stopping the bill; **knowing the patient's exact QMB/dual status is a gating intake question** that unlocks a near-automatic defense.
- **Source:** [NY AG, Oct 6 2022](https://ag.ny.gov/press-release/2022/attorney-general-james-secures-relief-patients-illegally-charged-ambulance) · [CFPB/CMS joint action, Oct 31 2024](https://www.consumerfinance.gov/about-us/newsroom/cfpb-and-cms-take-action-stop-illegal-billing-lowest-income-medicare-recipients/)

### 5.4 — Retroactive Medicaid eligibility: uninsured 86-day stay, $1M+ billed → Medicaid retro-pays; window is the whole game
- **Insurance:** uninsured at admission; **Medicaid applied for retroactively** (current rule: covers up to **3 months before** application). **Engine-relevant? No** (eligibility mechanics). _Documented in a Justice in Aging policy brief; patient "John" anonymized — advocacy material, not journalism._
- **Money:** charges **>$1,000,000**; Medicaid paid **$82,000** (FL's 45-inpatient-day cap); patient owed **~$0** once enrolled. Under FL's **proposed retro-eligibility waiver**, the hospital would've gotten **$0** — i.e. the patient could've faced the $1M.
- **What happened:** hospital staff took **65 days** to complete the application — nearly burning the entire 90-day retro window — but it landed in time and retro Medicaid worked as designed. **H.R. 1 (2026) shrinks the retro window to 1 month (expansion adults) / 2 months (others) starting Jan 2027** — a live, deteriorating clock.
- **Levers:** **file the Medicaid application immediately** (every day shrinks the retro window); know the **state's retro window** (and any waiver); **demand hospital financial-counselor application help** (they get paid → aligned incentive); once approved, send the Medicaid approval and have providers **re-bill for the retro period**; **fair-hearing appeal** on denial (≥90 days); charity care as the fallback if the window's blown.
- **Signal:** **timing is the lever** — retro Medicaid can erase a catastrophic uninsured bill, but only inside a shrinking window; **the agent's job is to start the clock and not let it lapse**; legal landscape is actively worsening (H.R. 1) → versioned knowledge.
- **Source:** [Justice in Aging, Sep 2019](https://www.justiceinaging.org/wp-content/uploads/2019/09/Medicaid-Retroactive-Coverage-Issue-Brief.pdf) · [NC Health News, Feb 6 2026](https://www.northcarolinahealthnews.org/2026/02/06/retroactive-medicaid-reduction/)

### 5.5 — Prior-auth denial of an emergency inpatient stay, $59,000 (⚠ Molina **ACA marketplace**, not Medicaid MCO — structural parallel)
- **Insurance:** **ACA marketplace plan (Molina) — NOT Medicaid managed care** (flagged: a named *Medicaid-MCO* prior-auth case is genuinely scarce; systemic evidence = OIG 2023, 12.5% MCO denial rate). Airlifted to an OON Phoenix hospital; Molina denied the **inpatient** stay for lack of prior auth. **Engine-relevant? No** (prior-auth / OON-emergency denial).
- **Money:** disputed **~$59,000** (direct patient bill $15,312.43); Molina paid **$0**; **unresolved** as of May 2026 (not yet in collections).
- **What happened:** Anderson (65) had transient global amnesia, was airlifted unconscious, discharged next day; Molina demanded prior auth she **couldn't have obtained while unconscious**. Multiple appeals + AZ & WA insurance-commissioner + congressional complaints over a year — **still open**.
- **Levers:** **ACA emergency-care protection** (42 CFR §147.138 — no prior auth for emergency services, incl. the immediate inpatient admission); **external independent medical review** (free, high overturn rate); **No Surprises Act** complaint (OON emergency; 1-800-985-3059); CMS/Healthcare.gov marketplace complaint; state-DOI; negotiate/charity care with a cooperative hospital.
- **Signal:** **prior-auth is wielded even where it's legally barred** (emergencies); the **same appeal→external-review→regulator ladder** as MA/commercial denials applies to Medicaid-MCO and ACA plans alike; **coverage-type precision matters** — the product must not confuse a Molina *ACA* plan with a Molina *Medicaid* plan, because the governing rules differ.
- **Source:** [KFF/WaPo, May 29 2026](https://kffhealthnews.org/health-care-costs/amnesia-arizona-hospital-prior-authorization-bill-of-the-month-may-2026/)

---
**Segment signals:** (1) **Medicaid & QMB create near-absolute $0 defenses (balance-billing prohibition, out-of-state emergency coverage) — but they're routinely violated and most low-income patients pay anyway.** The lever is *asserting a known right*, not auditing a charge → the agent must **ask the gating coverage question** (Medicaid? dual/QMB? which state?) up front, because it flips the entire playbook. (2) **Coordination-of-benefits is its own error class** (birthday rule, primary/secondary clawbacks) — fixable by correcting COB order, not by disputing charges; reconciling multiple payers' EOBs is exactly the amounts/activity-log job. (3) **Fully-insured vs self-funded (ERISA)** is load-bearing *again* (5.2) — it decides whether the state DOI or a DOL/ERISA appeal is your venue; **employer HR/broker** is a repeat decisive lever. (4) **Timing-as-lever** (retroactive Medicaid, 5.4): start the clock, don't let the window lapse — and the window is **shrinking by law (H.R. 1, 2027)**. (5) **Coverage-type precision is a product requirement** — Medicaid FFS vs Medicaid MCO vs dual/QMB vs ACA-marketplace each carry different rules; mislabeling sends the wrong lever. (6) **Press repeatedly resolves what the legal right alone didn't** (5.1), and engine relevance is **zero** across this segment.
