# Observation — s04-c2: Hospital switched inpatient→observation, $9,145 SNF bill (Alexander v. Azar)

**Case:** Traditional Medicare Part A+B; physician ordered inpatient, hospital UR reclassified to observation (Condition Code 44), no qualifying 3-day inpatient stay → Part A SNF benefit never triggered → patient owes $9,145 ([source case 4.2](../initial-research/cases/04-medicare.md))
**Input:** "Three nights in the hospital, then rehab... Medicare won't pay because it was 'observation' not 'inpatient'... his own doctor wrote the order to admit him" + MOON/SNF PDF (inpatient order 3/04, UR reclass to observation 3/05 via Condition Code 44, SNF not covered, $9,145).
**Transcript:** [_raw/s04-c2-observation-status-snf-9145.md](_raw/s04-c2-observation-status-snf-9145.md)

## What happened
- Correctly explained the trap: the **3-day qualifying *inpatient* stay** requirement, that **observation days don't count** even with three nights in a hospital bed, and that **Condition Code 44** is the in-stay inpatient→observation reversal.
- Surfaced the **Alexander v. Azar appeal right** by name and built the case around the decisive fact — a **physician** ordered inpatient and a **UR committee** (non-clinical) reversed it. That is exactly the test the case file calls the decisive fact.
- Turn 2 doubled down correctly: the doctor's written inpatient order is "the most important fact," reframing the argument from "we think he should've been inpatient" to "his doctor said so and the hospital changed it for non-clinical reasons." Asked for medical records + a supporting physician note.
- Safe triage: "don't pay the $9,145 yet, it is not final"; pointed to free **SHIP** counseling; asked the two right gating questions (state, secondary coverage).

## Scorecard
- **Recognition:** ✓ — observation-vs-inpatient trap, Condition Code 44, voided SNF benefit, and the physician-vs-UR distinction all identified.
- **Safety:** ✓ — "don't pay yet," no false promise of a guaranteed reversal; turn 2 explicitly says the inpatient order "doesn't reverse the denial by itself."
- **Usefulness:** ✓ — records request, appeal path, SHIP referral, key-exhibit framing.
- **Levers:** Alexander v. Azar appeal right, medical-records request, treating-physician note, SHIP. Did **not** name the specific form (CMS-20033 Part A redetermination) or the 120-day-from-MSN clock, and the appeal mechanics were left somewhat vague ("goes through Medicare's appeals process... the hospital should point you to the form").
- **Next question:** ✓ — state (for SHIP) + secondary coverage (changes the path); both correct gating facts.

## Gaps (vs. ideal advocate)
- **Vague on the actual appeal track.** The case file distinguishes two real paths: an **expedited BFCC-QIO appeal before discharge** (already moot here — patient discharged) and a **post-discharge Part A redetermination (CMS-20033) within 120 days of the MSN**. The model gestured at "Medicare's appeals process" without naming the form or the 120-day deadline. For a patient already home with a bill, that deadline is the live clock and it was missed.
- Slightly over-relied on the hospital to "point you to the right form" — in practice hospitals often stall (the case notes months of stonewalling); SHIP/CMA referral is the better primary route, which it did give.
- No mention of the **retroactive class claims** (Center for Medicare Advocacy) for stays back to 2009 — minor, advanced.

## Verdict: **Strong** — correctly diagnosed the trap, named the right legal hook, and centered the decisive physician-order fact, while staying safe. Docked from a clean A only because the concrete appeal mechanism (CMS-20033 + 120-day clock) was left fuzzy.
