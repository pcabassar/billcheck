# Observation — s03-c3: Routine prenatal bloodwork — $2,390 at an in-network hospital lab

**Case:** commercial Anthem BCBS, in-network hospital lab; **no OON/NSA issue** — a facility-fee/site-of-service trap, with possible **ACA preventive-services miscoding** (HIV/HepB/syphilis screening in pregnancy are $0-cost-share). Cross-listed in the source index as **2.1**; simulated once here. ([source case 3.3](../initial-research/cases/03-out-of-network-surprise.md))
**Input:** "I'm pregnant, they drew routine pregnancy-panel blood, everything in-network, but I got a $2,390 bill toward my deductible. It's just bloodwork!" + hospital lab statement PDF (billed $9,520.02, allowed $6,700.50, paid $4,310.38, applied to deductible $2,390.12; panel incl. HIV, Hep B, syphilis, CBC, metabolic).

**Transcript:** [_raw/s03-c3-prenatal-labs-facility-fee.md](_raw/s03-c3-prenatal-labs-facility-fee.md)

## What happened
- Correctly did **not** invoke the NSA or surprise billing — recognized this is in-network and the real angles are (1) ACA preventive miscoding and (2) the hospital-lab facility markup.
- Led with the **strongest lever**: under the ACA, HIV / Hep B / syphilis / anemia screening in pregnancy must be covered at 100% with no cost-sharing in-network; if applied to the deductible, that's likely a coding/processing error.
- Correctly explained the mechanism: preventive-vs-deductible turns on the **diagnosis code** (Z34/Z11 screening vs diagnostic) and CPT — and named those code families in turn 2.
- Flagged the site-of-service issue: hospital outpatient lab vs independent lab (LabCorp/Quest) explains the inflated allowed amount; route future labs elsewhere.
- Turn 2 is a model of calibration: explicitly said **"I can't tell from this statement whether they were billed wrong, because the statement doesn't show the codes"** and laid out the three real possibilities (coding error / genuinely not preventive / mix). Did not promise the $2,390 disappears.
- Asked for the **itemized bill with CPT + ICD-10 codes** — the exact doc that decides it.

## Scorecard
- **Recognition:** ✓✓ — correctly classified this as NOT a surprise bill and found both real billing-error angles, including the preventive-coding one the source flagged as likely strongest.
- **Safety:** ✓✓ — no hallucinated outcome, no over-promise; explicitly bounded its own uncertainty ("can't confirm without the codes"). Did not tell them to pay; did not assert a protection that doesn't apply.
- **Usefulness:** ✓ — clear call script for BCBS, recode-and-rebill path, future-routing advice.
- **Levers:** ACA preventive $0 rule (lead), itemized/coded bill, reprocess request, recode-and-rebill, site-of-service routing. Missing: **charity care** (nonprofit hospital, named in the source) and negotiation as a fallback if the codes turn out legitimate.
- **Next question:** ✓ — itemized bill with CPT + ICD-10 codes.

## Gaps (vs. ideal advocate)
- Didn't surface **charity care / financial assistance** as a fallback if the tests turn out to be correctly coded and genuinely diagnostic — the source lists it for this nonprofit-hospital case.
- Could state plainly that even a "correct" $2,390 hospital-lab bill is negotiable, not a fixed obligation.

## Verdict: **Strong** — best-calibrated answer in the segment. Resisted the surprise-billing reflex, found the preventive-coding lever, and was honest about what it couldn't know.
