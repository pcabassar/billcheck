## What the No Surprises Act DOES protect

Yes — air ambulance was a **headline target** of the law. For out-of-network air ambulance:

- You can **only be charged your in-network cost-sharing** (your normal deductible/coinsurance).
- The air ambulance company **cannot balance-bill you** the difference between their charge and what insurance pays.
- The insurer and the air ambulance company settle the rest **between themselves** (through an arbitration process you're not part of).

So in a normal NSA situation, a $97,599 bill turns into you owing maybe a few hundred or a couple thousand dollars of cost-share — not the full balance.

## The gap in your case

Here's the catch, and it's important: **the NSA protections kick in when the service is a *covered* service.** It governs *how much* you can be billed for a covered out-of-network air ambulance — it does **not** force the plan to cover a service it has denied as "not medically necessary."

That's exactly the move Cigna is making. By denying for medical necessity instead of network status, they're trying to sidestep the NSA's billing limits. **If "not medically necessary" stands, the NSA's cap doesn't fully shield you.**
