## The core problem with Cigna's logic

Cigna is treating "emergency" as meaning "came through the ER." That's not the legal standard. Emergency care is about the **medical condition**, not the door the patient walked through. Premature twins needing NICU intensive care is the textbook definition of an emergency medical condition — a newborn whose health is in serious jeopardy without immediate care. Originating in obstetrics rather than the ER doesn't change that.

So the "did not originate in the ER" reasoning is the weak point you attack.

## Two things may both apply here

**1. The No Surprises Act (NSA).** If your plan is subject to it (most are, as of 2022), federal law protects you when you get emergency services or care at an in-network facility where you couldn't choose an in-network provider. NICU care for newborns at the hospital where the mother delivered is exactly the kind of situation this was meant to cover — you can't shop for a network NICU mid-emergency. Under the NSA, you generally can't be balance-billed beyond your in-network cost-sharing.

**2. Your own plan's out-of-pocket maximum.** Notice the EOB lists an **OON out-of-pocket maximum of $10,000**. Even under their own out-of-network terms, your responsibility should cap there — not $80,084. The $80K figure suggests they're either applying balance billing (charging you the gap between billed charges and what they paid) or not applying the OOP max correctly. That's a billing/processing issue worth challenging on its own.

## What to do now, in order

1. **Get the babies' coverage details fully resolved.** Newborns are usually covered under the parent's plan for the first 30 days, then need to be enrolled. Confirm both babies were properly added — a coverage-effective-date glitch can cause exactly this "out of network newborn" flag.

2. **Deal with collections in writing.** Send the collection agency a letter saying you dispute the debt and are appealing it with insurance. This pauses aggressive collection and protects you while you fight.

3. **File a formal appeal with Cigna** — frame it as (a) this was an emergency medical condition regardless of admission source, and (b) the No Surprises Act applies, and (c) at minimum your $10,000 OOP max should cap your liability.

4. **Ask the hospital** whether they billed you under No Surprises Act protections and request an itemized bill.

## On whether the state can help — here's how to tell

This comes down to one question: **is your plan self-funded or fully insured?**

- **Fully insured** = your employer buys coverage from Cigna, and Cigna takes the financial risk. These plans are regulated by your **state insurance department**, so the state *can* help and you'd have a state appeal/complaint path.
- **Self-funded** = your employer pays the claims itself and just hires Cigna to administer them. These are regulated **federally** (under ERISA and the Department of Labor), not the state. The state insurance department generally can't intervene.

Here's the reassuring part: **the No Surprises Act covers both types.** So even if you're self-funded and the state can't help, you still have federal protection against this exact balance bill. You don't lose your strongest tool either way.

**How to find out which you have:**
- Look at your plan documents — the **Summary Plan Description** often says, or check whether benefits are "insured by Cigna" vs. "administered by Cigna."
- Or just **ask your employer's HR/benefits person**: "Is our medical plan self-funded or fully insured?" They'll know.

You actually have layered protection: the No Surprises Act (federal, applies regardless), your plan's own $10,000 out-of-pocket cap, and now a documented emergency for at least one twin. The $80K figure should not stand.
