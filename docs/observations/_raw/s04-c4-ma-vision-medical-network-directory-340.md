### billcheck
You're not necessarily stuck. Here's what's actually going on and what you can do.

## What happened

The clinic is genuinely in two different "networks" with your plan:
- **Vision network** – routine eye exams, glasses/contacts. The clinic IS in this one.
- **Medical network** – disease-based eye care like glaucoma. The clinic is NOT in this one.

A glaucoma evaluation is medical, so it got processed against the medical network and denied. This is a confusing distinction that the plan's own directory often doesn't make clear — and that's exactly the basis for your argument.

## Why you have a real case

If the plan's provider search tool showed a green "in-network" checkmark and didn't make clear it only applied to vision, you reasonably relied on inaccurate plan information. Medicare Advantage plans are required to maintain accurate provider directories, and "the directory misled me" is a legitimate appeal argument. Plans frequently waive the extra cost or pay at the in-network rate in these situations.

## What to do — two tracks

**1. Appeal to UnitedHealthcare (most important).**
Medicare Advantage gives you formal appeal rights. File a written appeal (called a "reconsideration") within **60 days** of the denial. Your argument:
- The plan's online directory listed Lakeshore Eye Clinic as in-network with no indication the listing was vision-only.
- You relied on that information in good faith before the visit.
- Ask the plan to process the claim at the in-network rate or waive the balance.

**2. Document the directory listing.**
This is the key piece. If you can still pull up the clinic on the plan's website, take a screenshot showing the in-network checkmark and the date. If it's no longer showing or has changed, note that too. This evidence makes your appeal much stronger.

## Missing the screenshot isn't fatal

A few things work in your favor even without it:

- **The plan has its own records.** Directories are dated and often archived. In your appeal you can state you checked the tool before your visit and it showed the clinic in-network — the plan can verify what its own directory said.
- **The burden isn't entirely on you.** Medicare Advantage plans are required to keep accurate directories. If theirs was misleading (showing a vision-only listing as plain "in-network"), that's their problem to answer for, not just yours to prove.
- **You can still check now.** Go look today. If the listing still doesn't clearly separate "vision" from "medical," that current state supports that it was misleading when you looked. Screenshot it now even if it's late.

## Is $340 worth it?

For most people on a fixed income, yes — and here's the practical math:

- **The appeal is free.** It costs you a letter and a stamp (or an online submission). No filing fee, no risk.
- **The odds are reasonable.** "Your directory misled me" is one of the more winnable appeal arguments, because the accuracy requirement is squarely on the plan.
- **The downside is just… the current bill.** If you lose, you're no worse off than now. You can still set up a payment plan with the clinic at that point.

A low-effort, no-cost shot at erasing $340 is worth taking.
