First, take a breath. A million-dollar balance is terrifying, but you almost certainly will not pay anything close to that. There are real, established paths here, and the most important one is already in motion.

## The big thing: that Medicaid application can pay these bills

Here's the part most people don't know: **Medicaid has retroactive coverage.** In most states, if someone qualifies, Medicaid can pay bills going back **up to 3 months before the application date** — even though he wasn't enrolled when he was admitted.

The hospital already started his application on day 65, and it's still pending. If he's approved, Medicaid can cover this entire stay. So it is **not too late** — this is exactly how it's supposed to work for uninsured hospital patients.

One Florida-specific catch I want to flag: **Florida did not expand Medicaid**, so adults without dependent children, a disability, or being 65+ can have a hard time qualifying on income alone. So the key question is what category your dad falls into.

## What I need to know to point you right

1. **How old is your dad?** (If 65+, Medicare may also be an option, even retroactively in some cases.)
2. **Does he have a disability,** or was this hospitalization tied to a condition that's left him disabled?
3. **What's his rough monthly income and what does he have in assets?** (Florida Medicaid has strict limits.)

These three answers determine which door is most likely to open.

## What to do right now (regardless)

- **Do not pay anything yet, and don't agree to a payment plan.** Doing so can undercut other options.
- **Stay on top of the pending Medicaid application.** Ask the hospital's financial counseling office (the people who filed it) for the application date and current status, and whether they need any documents from your dad to finish it. Pending applications often stall waiting on paperwork.
- **If Medicaid is denied or he doesn't qualify, ask for the hospital's financial assistance / charity care policy.** Most hospitals (and many are legally required to) write off bills entirely or heavily for low-income, uninsured patients. This is a separate safety net from Medicaid.

## The short version
