### billcheck
This is a strong case for you, and the timestamp on that consent form is the key. Here's why and what to do.

## Why you're in a good position

You used an **in-network facility/system** but got an **out-of-network doctor** you didn't choose — that's exactly the surprise-billing scenario the federal **No Surprises Act (NSA)** was written to stop.

Under the NSA, a provider can only bill you out-of-network rates if you sign a valid consent (a "Surprise Billing Protection Form") **before** care is delivered, with specific timing requirements (generally given at least 72 hours ahead, or 3 hours before for same-day care). A consent signed at 4:15 PM for a visit that ended at 2:42 PM is **invalid on its face** — and they've already admitted it isn't allowed.

When valid consent isn't obtained, you can only be billed the **in-network cost-sharing amount** — which lines up with the ~$60 the app quoted you. The provider is supposed to chase the rest from your insurer, not you.

(One thing I can't confirm from the document: whether your specific plan is NSA-covered. Most are, but some self-funded employer plans and certain government plans work differently. What kind of insurance do you have — employer plan, marketplace/ACA, Medicaid, etc.?)

## What to do now

**1. Put it in writing to Mount Sinai billing.** Stop relying on phone calls. Send a short letter/email stating:
- The visit was at an in-network facility with an out-of-network rendering physician.
- No valid No Surprises Act consent was obtained — the consent is timestamped **4:15 PM, after the 2:42 PM visit ended** — and their staff **admitted** this is not allowed.
- You are disputing the $660 and will pay only the in-network cost-sharing (~$60 as quoted in the app).
- Request a corrected bill and written confirmation.

**2. File a federal No Surprises Act complaint.** Call **1-800-985-3059** or file at **cms.gov/nosurprises**. This is free and often the fastest lever — providers respond quickly once CMS is involved.

**3. Also file with NY.** New York has its own strong surprise-billing law and an **Independent Dispute Resolution** process. Contact the **NY Department of Financial Services** (dfs.ny.gov). Telehealth billed through a NY system likely falls under NY protections too.

**4. Loop in your insurer.** Ask them to confirm in writing that the facility was in-network and to flag this as a surprise bill. Their pressure helps.

**5. Protect your credit.** It's been a year — if this is sent to collections, the NSA dispute and the admitted-invalid consent are your defense. Keep every record.
