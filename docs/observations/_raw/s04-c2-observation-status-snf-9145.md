### billcheck
I'm sorry you're dealing with this — it's one of the most frustrating quirks in Medicare, and what happened to your dad is unfortunately common. Let me explain what's going on and, more importantly, what you can actually do about it.

## Why this happened

You're right that his own doctor wrote an **inpatient** admit order on 3/04. But the next day (3/05), the hospital's **Utilization Review** team reversed that decision and reclassified him to **observation (outpatient)** using something called **Condition Code 44**. That's a process hospitals use to change a patient from inpatient to outpatient *while they're still in the hospital*.

Here's the painful part: Medicare's SNF benefit requires a **3-day qualifying *inpatient* stay** (3 midnights as a formally admitted inpatient). Observation days — even though he was in a hospital bed for three nights — **don't count.** So Medicare denied the rehab coverage, and the SNF is billing your dad the full $9,145.

This is exactly the situation the MOON notice warned about. It feels like a technicality because it *is* one — and it's a well-known problem.

## The good news: you have real options now

A 2020 federal court ruling (*Alexander v. Azar*) and a recent CMS process give Medicare patients the **right to appeal an observation reclassification** — specifically when a doctor ordered inpatient and the hospital changed it. This is newer and many people (and even some hospital staff) don't know about it. Your dad may qualify because:

- His **doctor ordered inpatient admission** (you have it in writing)
- The hospital **reclassified him to observation**
- He had a **3-night stay**
- He was **denied SNF coverage** as a result

That combination is the textbook case this appeal right was created for.

## What I'd do, in order

1. **Don't pay the $9,145 yet.** It is not "final."

2. **Request your dad's full medical records** from St. Catherine — especially the physician's inpatient order and any utilization review notes. You'll want the documentation of *why* they thought observation was appropriate.

3. **File the observation status appeal.** This goes through Medicare's appeals process (not the hospital). The hospital should be able to point you to the right form, but if they stall, you can contact your **State Health Insurance Assistance Program (SHIP)** — free, one-on-one Medicare counseling — to help you file.

4. **Call your SHIP now.** They handle exactly this and the help is free. Tell me your dad's state and I'll point you to the right number.

## How it strengthens your appeal

In the observation-status appeal, the central question is essentially: *should this patient have been an inpatient?* Having the treating doctor's own written inpatient order is strong evidence that the answer is yes. It shifts the argument from "we think he should have been inpatient" to "his doctor said he should be, and the hospital changed it for non-clinical reasons."

A few things that will make this even stronger:

- **The medical records** showing *why* the doctor admitted him (severity of the fall, his age, any complications, fall risk, inability to care for himself). At 94 with a fall serious enough to need 3 nights and rehab, the clinical case for inpatient is usually solid.
- **Any note from the doctor** stating he or she believed inpatient was medically necessary. If the doctor is willing to write a brief letter supporting that, it carries real weight.

It doesn't reverse the denial by itself — the SNF bill stays on hold until the appeal is decided. But it gives you a genuinely strong foundation rather than a long-shot. Keep that paperwork safe; it's your key exhibit.
