Under the Affordable Care Act, certain prenatal screenings are required to be covered at **100% with no cost-sharing** when you're in-network — meaning no deductible, no copay. The screenings on your statement are exactly the kind that often qualify:

- **HIV screening** — ACA preventive for pregnant women

If those were processed against your deductible instead of as free preventive care, that's likely a **coding or processing error**, and you shouldn't owe anything for those components.

The catch is *how* it was coded. Preventive coverage depends on the diagnosis code (a routine screening code vs. a diagnostic code) and the procedure codes. If your OB's order was coded as "screening," BCBS should have paid these at 100%.

Routine prenatal bloodwork run through a hospital lab is dramatically more expensive than the same panel at an independent lab (LabCorp, Quest). ## Why it comes down to the codes

Whether insurance treats a test as "free preventive" vs. "applied to deductible" hinges on two things:

1. **The diagnosis code (ICD-10)** — Was it coded as routine screening (e.g., a Z34 "supervision of normal pregnancy" or Z11 screening code)? Or was it coded as diagnostic, like investigating a specific problem? Screening codes trigger preventive coverage. Diagnostic codes go to your deductible.

2. **The procedure codes (CPT)** — These have to match the preventive list.

If the lab attached the wrong diagnosis code, your insurer wouldn't recognize the test as preventive — and it would dump it onto your deductible, exactly like what happened here.
