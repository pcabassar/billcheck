I know this feels backwards — paid bill, $0 balance, and yet a lawsuit. Let me explain what's likely going on and, more importantly, what you need to do right now.

## The most important thing first: the 21-day clock

**You must file a written response (an "Answer") with the court within 21 days of being served.** This is the single thing that matters most. If you miss it, Credit Service Company can get a *default judgment* — meaning they win automatically, no matter how good your case is, and can then garnish wages or hit your credit. Don't skip this even though the bill looks paid.

The court that issued this (El Paso County Court) has an Answer form. You file it, check the boxes that you dispute the debt, and you can mail a copy to the plaintiff. There's usually a small filing fee that can be waived if money's tight.

## What's probably happening

A few likely explanations — and they're not mutually exclusive:

- **A separate bill from the ER visit.** ER visits often generate *multiple* bills: the hospital facility, and separately the ER physicians, radiologists, or a lab — often outside companies. Your hospital portal showing $0 only covers the *hospital's* portion. One of those other providers may not have been paid and sold the account to Credit Service Company (a debt buyer/collector — that's why the hospital's name isn't on it).
- **A billing or insurance gap** on one of those provider bills (claim denied, sent to wrong insurance, balance-billed).
- **Mistaken identity or an already-resolved account** sold anyway.

"Credit Service Company (assignee)" tells us they bought or were assigned someone's account. The filing doesn't even say whose — and in many places, **they have to prove they own the debt and what it's for.** A complaint with no named original creditor is weak.

2. **Gather proof.** Your insurance EOB(s) for the 03/02/2024 visit, and the portal $0 balance. The EOB will show exactly which providers were paid and how much.
3. **Call your insurer** and ask: for date of service 03/02/2024, were there *separate claims* from ER physicians, radiology, anesthesia, or labs — and were any denied or left with a patient balance?
4. **File your Answer** before the deadline, disputing the debt (paid/no proof of ownership/wrong amount).

### billcheck
**File with the court. Don't rely on a phone call.**

Here's the hard truth: a phone call to Credit Service Company does *nothing* to protect you legally. Even if the person on the phone agrees with you, that doesn't stop the lawsuit, and it doesn't stop a default judgment. Only a written **Answer filed with the court** within 21 days does that.

Think of it as two separate tracks:

- **The court track** — the only one with the 21-day deadline and real consequences. This is mandatory.
- **The talking-to-them track** — optional, and frankly risky to do alone right now (more below).

## Why not just call them

1. **It doesn't stop the clock.** The 21 days keeps running. Miss it and you lose by default even if you're 100% right.
2. **Anything you say can be used against you.** If you say something like "oh yeah, I remember that visit" or "can I set up a payment plan," that can be treated as acknowledging the debt — which can hurt you and even restart time limits. Debt buyers count on people calling and talking themselves into trouble.
3. **They often can't prove their case.** A debt buyer with no named original creditor frequently can't produce the documents showing they own the debt and what it's for. Making them prove it in court is often your strongest position. A phone call lets them skip that.

## So the order of operations is:

1. **File the Answer with the court first** (within 21 days). This is non-negotiable.
2. In the Answer, dispute the debt — that it's paid, that they haven't shown they own it, and that the amount/original creditor isn't even stated.
3. *Then,* with your case protected, you can deal with the substance — get your EOB, confirm whether some sub-provider bill slipped through, and decide whether to negotiate.

If you do talk to anyone, do it **in writing**, and don't admit you owe anything.

You're not going to mess this up if you hit the deadline. So let's nail that down — **when were you served the papers?** That tells us your exact deadline, and then I'll walk you through filling out the Answer line by line.
