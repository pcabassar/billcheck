Here's what's actually going on, because it matters for how you push back.

**What they billed**

They coded your "matching call" as **CPT 90834 — psychotherapy, 45 minutes**. That's a real therapy session code. It got applied entirely to your deductible, which is why you owe the full $179.

**Why their "legally unable to refund" line is the key issue**

Here's the catch they're not explaining: a provider can't just refund you for a service they billed insurance for. Once they submit a claim to your insurer saying "we provided a 45-minute psychotherapy session," they're locked into that claim. Refunding you separately while keeping the claim on file would be improper. *That's* what "legally unable" means.

But that doesn't mean you're stuck. It means the fix isn't a refund — **it's getting them to retract/correct the claim with your insurer.** If they reverse the claim, the $179 comes back off your deductible and you owe nothing. That's the outcome you actually want.

**The real question: was this a therapy session?**

A matching/intake call that pairs you with a therapist is generally *not* a 90834 psychotherapy session. 90834 requires an actual treatment session with a licensed clinician doing therapy. If a coordinator did a 45-minute intake/matching call, billing it as 90834 is likely **miscoding** — and that's your strongest argument. You can't deliver therapy and then not assign a therapist for three weeks.

**What to do**

1. **Reframe your ask.** Stop asking for a refund. Tell them in writing: *"I'm asking you to retract or correct the claim billed to my insurer as CPT 90834 on 05/12/2026. This was a matching/intake call, not a psychotherapy session, and I never consented to a billable clinical visit."*

2. **Pin down who you actually spoke with.** Was it a licensed therapist or a coordinator? That single fact determines whether 90834 is defensible.

3. **File a dispute with your insurer** in parallel — report the 90834 charge as a service you didn't receive. Insurers take "I was billed for a therapy session that never happened" seriously, and they can lean on the provider to correct it.

4. **Watch for surprise-billing/consent issues.** If you were never told this call was billable, that's a consent problem worth raising — but the coding angle is cleaner and faster.

**The part that's a dodge:** "Legally unable to refund" is *not* the same as "you owe this and there's nothing we can do." They absolutely can:

- **Retract or correct the claim** with your insurer (this is routine — providers void/correct claims all the time)
- **Reverse the charge** if the claim comes off
- **Recode it** to something non-billable, or
- **Adjust your balance** to zero

They're hiding behind the narrow refund mechanics to avoid doing the thing that actually fixes it — pulling the claim. That's a process problem, not a legal one.
