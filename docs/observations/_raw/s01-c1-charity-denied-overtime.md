## The biggest point first: you may have been wrongly denied

You said you were denied because you made "about $200 too much" — and that overage came from one month of overtime. That detail matters a lot. Both programs have rules that may let you back in:

**Colorado law (HB21-1198) — this is your strongest card.** Colorado has a hospital discounted care law that requires hospitals to screen uninsured patients for assistance and cap what they can charge. Under this law, if you're at or under **250% of the Federal Poverty Level**, the hospital legally **cannot charge you more than the lowest negotiated rate they accept from insurers** — and your monthly payments are capped at **4% of your monthly income**, with the total debt forgiven after 36 months of payments. That's a world away from $80,232.

A $200 overage is so small that I'd push hard on this. A few things to check:
- **CICP and the discounted care law use different income limits.** Being over the CICP line doesn't automatically put you over the discounted-care line. Confirm which one you were actually screened for.
- **How did they count your income?** Many programs let you use *current* monthly income (annualized) rather than last year's tax return. If you're no longer getting that overtime, your real ongoing income may put you under the limit. Ask them exactly what figure they used and what period it covered.

## What I need from you to point you precisely

1. What was your approximate **total household income** last year, and how many people are in your household?
2. Do you have the **denial letters**? They should state the income figure they used and the limit you exceeded.
3. Was the denial from the **hospital's own charity policy**, **CICP**, or the **state discounted care program** — or some combination?

That'll tell me whether you're genuinely over the line or whether they used the wrong number.

## In the meantime — things that are true regardless

- **Don't pay anything or agree to a payment plan yet.** Agreeing to terms can undercut your appeal.
- **Appeal the denial in writing**, and specifically ask them to recalculate using current monthly income and to confirm they screened you under the HB21-1198 discounted care program, not just CICP.
- **That $80,232 is the "chargemaster" sticker price** — a fantasy number almost no one actually pays. Insurers pay a fraction of it. Even setting aside the assistance programs, an uninsured patient can usually negotiate this down dramatically.
- **You can request an itemized bill.** This statement is just two line items; the detailed version often contains errors and duplicate charges, especially on a complicated two-surgery stay.

Want me to draft the appeal letter for you? If you can tell me your household income and size, I can also tell you roughly where you fall on the poverty-level scale and whether they likely got the denial wrong.

**1. Call the VA about emergency care reimbursement.** This is potentially huge. The VA can sometimes pay for **emergency care at a non-VA hospital** for veterans — even when the VA didn't pre-authorize it, because nobody calls ahead during a burst appendix. - There's a **notification deadline** — generally the VA wants to be notified within **72 hours** of emergency care. You're past that now, but **do not assume you're disqualified** — deadlines can be extended for good cause, and you should file anyway. The clock is the reason this is your #1 call.
