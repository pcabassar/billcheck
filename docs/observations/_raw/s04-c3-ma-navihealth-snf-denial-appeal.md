## What this notice actually is

This is a **Notice of Medicare Non-Coverage (NOMNC)**. It's a standard form the plan must give you before cutting off skilled nursing coverage. The key thing it's telling you: **you have the right to a free "fast appeal"** through an outside reviewer called the **QIO (Quality Improvement Organization)** — not the plan itself. The QIO is independent of UnitedHealthcare and naviHealth.

## The deadline is the urgent part

You must call the QIO number on the notice **by noon the day before coverage ends.** Coverage ends 03/26, so you need to call **by noon on 03/25.** Call today — don't wait.

When you call, just say: **"I want to file a fast appeal of the discharge notice."** That's all you need to say. They'll take it from there.

## Why this is worth doing

- The appeal is **free**.
- While the QIO reviews it, **he can stay and you are not charged** for the disputed days, even past day 21, as long as you filed on time.
- If you **win**, coverage continues.
- If you **lose**, you're generally only responsible for costs starting **after** the QIO's decision date — not retroactively. So filing protects you financially even if it doesn't go your way.

That $70,000 figure on the notice does **not** apply while the appeal is pending.

## What helps you win

Your husband's situation is exactly what these appeals are for. The plan's reason ("goals substantially met") is contradicted by what his doctor and PT are telling you. So:

1. **Today:** Ask his doctor and physical therapist to put **in writing** that he still needs skilled-level rehab — that he can't walk or dress independently and needs more time. The QIO will request his records, but a clear note from his treating providers carries real weight.
2. The QIO typically decides within about **72 hours** of your call.

## One thing to know about Medicare Advantage

Skilled nursing under traditional Medicare can be covered up to **100 days** per benefit period when medically necessary. With Medicare Advantage plans, a third party like naviHealth often reviews and cuts coverage early. These early cutoffs are appealed successfully fairly often, especially when the patient clearly still needs care — which is your situation.

Yes — getting that in writing from his neurologist would help a lot. She's a treating physician disagreeing directly with the plan's "goals substantially met" reasoning, and that's exactly the kind of evidence the QIO weighs heavily.

## What the letter should say

Keep it simple and factual. Ideally it states:

- That she is his **treating neurologist**
- That in her medical opinion he **still requires skilled-level care** and is **not ready for discharge**
- **Specifics**: he can't walk independently, can't dress himself, the stroke deficits, and that he needs continued skilled rehab
- That stopping skilled care now would be **medically inappropriate / unsafe**
- An estimate of **how much more time** he needs (the 3 weeks his providers mentioned)

It doesn't have to be long or formal — a signed note on letterhead is fine. The clearer and more specific, the better.

## Who to give it to

1. **The QIO** — this is the main one. Once you call and start the fast appeal, ask them how they want it (usually fax or a secure upload). Get the fax number or instructions when you're on the phone with them.
2. **The SNF's case manager / social worker** — give them a copy too. They send records to the QIO and can route it to the right place, and it helps to have it in his chart.

## Timing matters most

The QIO often decides within ~72 hours, so the letter is most useful if it gets in **early**. Two things to do today:

- **Make the QIO call first** — don't wait for the letter to start the appeal. The call is what protects him from charges. The letter is supporting evidence you add right after.
- **Ask the neurologist's office to write it now** and either fax it straight to the QIO or hand it to the SNF case manager today/tomorrow.

If the neurologist's office is slow, even a brief signed note faxed promptly beats a polished letter that arrives after the decision.
