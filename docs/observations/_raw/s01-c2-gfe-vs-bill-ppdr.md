## The key thing: the No Surprises Act protects you

Because you're uninsured and got a written **Good Faith Estimate (GFE)**, federal law gives you a real tool. If your final bill is **$400 or more above the estimate**, you can dispute it through a formal **Patient-Provider Dispute Resolution (PPDR)** process.

Your bill is **$47,438 over** the estimate ($97,998 vs. $50,560). That's not a close call — it's nearly double, far past the $400 threshold.

## "Complications" isn't an automatic free pass

A hospital can bill more than the estimate, but they generally have to show the extra charges were *unforeseeable* and not something that should have been included up front. A vague phone explanation of "complications" doesn't meet that bar. In the dispute process, the burden is on **them** to justify the difference — not on you to disprove it.

One number jumps out: **surgical implants/supplies = $50,140**, which is basically the *entire* original estimate by itself. It's worth asking whether implants were accounted for in the GFE at all, because if a planned surgery's main implant was left out of the estimate, that's an estimate problem, not a complication.

## What to do now — a few steps

1. **Don't pay or agree to a payment plan yet.** Agreeing can undercut your dispute.
2. **File the dispute.** You generally have **120 days from the date of the bill** to start the PPDR process through CMS. There's a small fee (around $25) that you may get back if you win. I can walk you through filing.
3. **Separately, ask about financial assistance / charity care.** Many hospitals (especially nonprofits) must offer this to uninsured patients, and it can wipe out or sharply cut the bill regardless of the dispute.

## Step 1: Get it in writing from the hospital first
Before the formal dispute, send a **written request** (email or letter — keep a copy) asking them to:
- Provide an **itemized, line-by-line bill** with billing codes (CPT/HCPCS).

Why this first: it forces them to commit to an explanation on paper, and sometimes the bill gets corrected at this stage without a formal fight. It also strengthens your dispute if you do file.

## Step 2: File the federal dispute (PPDR) if they don't resolve it
This is the "real process" you're asking about. It's run by **CMS** (the federal Medicare/Medicaid agency), not the hospital:
- Go to **cms.gov/nosurprises** → Patient-Provider Dispute Resolution.
- File **online or by mail**, within **120 days of the bill date**.
- Fee is about **$25** (waived/refunded in hardship or if you win).
- A neutral third party reviews it. The hospital has to justify the extra charges. While it's under review, they **can't send you to collections** on the disputed amount.

## Step 3 (in parallel): Apply for financial assistance / charity care
This is separate and can help no matter how the dispute turns out. Ask the hospital's billing office for their **financial assistance application**.
