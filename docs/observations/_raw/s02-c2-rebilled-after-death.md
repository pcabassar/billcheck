### billcheck
I'm sorry you're dealing with this so long after losing your husband. Let me explain what I'm seeing and what to do.

## What this bill does — and doesn't — explain

The hospital is saying your "patient responsibility" jumped from $823.15 to $1,916.31. But **this statement gives you no reason why.** It just asserts a higher number 14 months later. That's the core problem: a revised bill with no explanation isn't something you should pay on faith.

There are only a few legitimate reasons a patient share goes *up* after the fact:
- The insurer reprocessed the claim and reduced what it covered (a "clawback" or adjustment).
- A billing error was corrected.
- Something was reassigned from "insurance pays" to "you pay."

But there are also reasons it could be **wrong**, including a hospital billing mistake or a re-bill that BCBS never actually adjusted.

## The one document that settles this

**You need the EOB (Explanation of Benefits) from Blue Cross Blue Shield of Illinois.** The EOB — not the hospital bill — is the authority on what you actually owe. It will show the allowed amount, what insurance paid, and "patient responsibility."

Here's the key test:
- If the **current BCBS EOB also shows $1,916.31** as your responsibility → the charge is probably legitimate, though you can still ask why it changed.
- If the BCBS EOB shows your responsibility is still **$823.15** → the hospital is billing you more than your insurer says you owe, and you do **not** pay it. That would likely be a balance-billing problem (improper for an in-network hospital).

Do you have the original EOB from March 2025, and have you received any newer EOB from BCBS? That's the thing that moves this forward.

## A few important points for your situation

1. **Don't pay yet.** Paying now could waive your ability to dispute it.

2. **The debt belongs to the estate, not automatically to you.** You signed as "guarantor," which can create personal liability — but that varies. If your husband's estate was small or already closed, and depending on Illinois rules, you may have limited or no personal obligation. Worth knowing before you pay anything personal.

3. **Watch the clock on the EOB appeal.** If BCBS reprocessed the claim and reduced coverage, you have a right to appeal — but there are deadlines from the date of *their* decision.

## What I'd do, in order

1. Call BCBS of Illinois and ask: "Has the claim for this March 2025 stay been reprocessed? What does the most recent EOB show as patient responsibility?" Get them to send/confirm the current EOB.
2. Call Barnes Health billing and ask them to send **an itemized explanation of what changed** and a copy of the EOB they're relying on.
3. Compare the two.

Can you tell me whether you've gotten any new EOB from BCBS, and whether your husband's estate went through probate? Those two answers will shape your best move.

This matters: an account flagged as **in dispute** generally won't get sent to collections while it's being worked. Get the rep's name and write down the date.

## Step 2: Follow up in writing

A phone call alone isn't proof. Send a short letter or message through their patient portal saying the same thing — that you dispute the charge and are requesting documentation. **Keep a copy.** This creates a paper trail if it ever does reach collections.

## Step 3: Call BCBS for the current EOB

Ask what their most recent EOB shows as your responsibility for this stay. This is the deciding document (from my earlier note).

## Step 4: Compare and respond

- **EOB still says $823.15** → you owe nothing more. Send the hospital the EOB and your paid receipt; tell them to correct the account.
