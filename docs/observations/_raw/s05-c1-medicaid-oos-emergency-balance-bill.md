## What's actually going on

Two things on this statement matter:

1. **"Guarantor insurance on file: NONE"** — The hospital has no record of your Medicaid. So they never submitted a claim. They're billing you as if you're uninsured. That's the main problem, and it's fixable.

2. **"This facility is not enrolled with Florida Medicaid"** — This is the part the billing lady is leaning on. It's true that Florida Medicaid generally only pays in-state providers. **But there's a specific exception for emergencies out of state.**

## The rule that helps you

Federal Medicaid law requires your state to cover **emergency services** received out of state — and a heart attack (acute MI) is about as clear an emergency as exists. Florida Medicaid can pay an out-of-state hospital for a true emergency even if that hospital isn't normally enrolled. The hospital usually has to enroll as a one-time/out-of-state provider to get paid, and many hospitals don't bother to chase this — they just bill the patient instead. That appears to be what happened here.

So "we're not enrolled with Florida Medicaid" is **not** the same as "you owe this." It means the claim hasn't been routed correctly.

## What to do — in order

1. **Tell the hospital in writing to stop billing you and submit to Florida Medicaid.** Give them your Medicaid ID. Say clearly: this was an emergency admission for an acute MI, and you want them to bill your Medicaid as an out-of-state emergency. Ask them to place the account on hold while this is sorted out. Do this in writing (email or letter) so you have a record.

2. **Call Florida Medicaid yourself** and report an out-of-state emergency that hasn't been billed. Ask them exactly what the hospital needs to do to file the claim and whether they can do **retroactive** authorization. Note: emergency Medicaid claims often have a filing deadline (commonly 12 months from the date of service — your service was 12/25/2025, so you have time, but don't sit on it).

3. **Do not agree to any payment plan in the meantime.** Signing one can be treated as you accepting the debt as yours.

## Why they're wrong

- Federal Medicaid law (42 CFR 431.52) **requires** states to cover emergency care their members get out of state.
- An out-of-state hospital that isn't normally enrolled can enroll as a **one-time / out-of-state provider** specifically to bill for that emergency. This is a routine process — large hospital systems like Monument Health do it. The front-line billing rep just may not know how, or doesn't want the hassle.
