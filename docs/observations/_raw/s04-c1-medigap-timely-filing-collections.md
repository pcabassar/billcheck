### billcheck
Take a breath — based on this Medicare notice, you very likely do **not** owe this $3,000.

## Why

Three things on this notice protect you:

1. **"Maximum you may be billed: $0.00"** — This is Medicare telling you, in writing, that you cannot legally be billed for this. This is the key line.

2. **Denial reason CO-29 (timely filing).** The anesthesia group sat on this claim. The date of service was 10/03/2024, but Medicare didn't receive the claim until 03/12/2026 — about 17 months later. Medicare's filing deadline is **one year** from the date of service. They blew it.

3. **The provider accepted assignment.** When a provider accepts assignment and the claim is denied for *their own* late filing, federal rules prohibit them from billing the patient for that amount. The financial loss falls on the provider, not on you. This is exactly what the "CO" prefix in CO-29 means — "CO" = **C**ontractual **O**bligation, i.e., the provider eats it, not the patient.

So this is not your debt to pay, and it shouldn't be in collections.

## What to do now

**1. Don't pay it, and don't ignore it.** Collection accounts can be reported to credit bureaus, so you need to respond — in writing.

**2. Send the collection agency a written dispute** (mail it, keep a copy). Say plainly: this claim was denied by Medicare for the provider's untimely filing under CO-29, the provider accepted assignment, the Medicare Summary Notice states "Maximum you may be billed: $0.00," and therefore the patient cannot be held liable. Demand they cease collection and not report it. Include a copy of this MSN.

Do this within **30 days** of their first letter to fully preserve your dispute rights.

**3. Optionally call the anesthesia group too** and tell them the same thing — that the MSN bars patient billing.

### billcheck
Good instinct. The main rule: **never say "I'll pay something" or "let me pay part of it"** — even agreeing to a small payment can reset the clock and be treated as admitting the debt is yours. Keep it factual and brief.

## What to say on the call

Stay calm and stick to facts:

> "I'm calling about account [number]. I'm disputing this debt in full. Medicare denied this claim because the provider filed it too late — denial code CO-29 — and the provider accepted assignment. My Medicare Summary Notice states the maximum I can be billed is zero dollars. Under Medicare's rules, the patient cannot be held responsible for a claim denied for the provider's untimely filing. I do not owe this and I'm disputing it in writing."

Then:

> "Please note the account as disputed, and do not report this to the credit bureaus while it's in dispute. What's the address to send my written dispute and supporting documentation?"

## Things to do / not do

- **Do** get the rep's name and write down the date/time of the call.
- **Do** ask for their mailing address so you can send the written dispute (the call doesn't replace the letter — the letter is what protects you).
- **Don't** say "I owe," "I can't afford it," or "can I set up a payment plan." All of those imply it's your debt.
- **Don't** give them new personal or bank information.
- **Don't** get drawn into arguing. If they push back, just repeat: *"The Medicare notice says I can't be billed. I'm disputing this in writing."*

## Important
The phone call is a courtesy and a paper trail. **The written dispute letter (sent by mail, with a copy of the MSN, ideally certified) is what actually protects your rights and credit.** Don't skip it.
