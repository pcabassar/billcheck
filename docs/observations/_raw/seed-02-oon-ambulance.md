### billcheck
Here's what's happening with your bill, Peggy.

## What this $2,711 actually is

This is a **balance bill**. The ambulance company charged $3,606. Your insurance decided $895 was "reasonable and customary" and paid that. The ambulance is now billing you for the leftover $2,711 — the gap between what they charged and what insurance allowed.

Your siblings likely paid less because their ambulance companies were either in-network or simply charged closer to what insurance considers reasonable. Same crash, different providers, very different bills — unfortunately that's common and not by itself illegal.

## The key question: are you protected?

This is where it matters a lot.

**Ground ambulances are NOT covered by the federal No Surprises Act.** That's the big gap in the law — air ambulances and most ER care are protected from balance billing, but ground ambulances were specifically left out. So federal law probably doesn't help you here.

**BUT — many states have their own ground-ambulance balance-billing protections,** and this matters because Valley Fire District is a **government/municipal provider**. Some states bar public ambulance services from balance billing insured patients at all, or cap what they can charge.

So the single most important thing I need: **what state did this happen in?** That determines whether you legally owe this or not.

## A few things that stand out regardless

- The charge math is odd: $3,606 total, but ALS base + 12 miles @ $30 = $360 in mileage. That implies a ~$3,246 base rate, which is high. Worth scrutinizing.
- "Out of network" for an *emergency* ambulance you didn't choose is exactly the kind of situation many state laws were written to address.

## What to do right now

1. **Don't pay yet.** Paying can waive some rights.
2. Tell me the **state**, and I'll tell you whether you're protected.
3. **Check if this is a car-accident claim** — was there auto insurance involved (your own MedPay/PIP, or the at-fault driver's)? In a crash, the *ambulance bill often gets paid by auto coverage, not health insurance*. This is huge and may be the real answer.

What state was the accident in, and did anyone file an auto insurance claim?

## 1. Route it through auto insurance (often the biggest win)
In a car accident, the ambulance bill frequently shouldn't go through health insurance at all.
- **Your own MedPay or PIP coverage** (if you have it) often pays ambulance and ER bills *with no balance billing* — sometimes in full.
- **The at-fault driver's liability** can also cover it.
- Action: Tell me if any auto claim was opened, and whether your policy has MedPay/PIP. This alone could erase the $2,711.

## 2. Check your state's balance-billing law
Because Valley Fire District is a **government provider**, some states bar them from balance billing insured patients entirely. Action: give me the **state** and I'll tell you if you're protected.

## 3. Appeal to your health insurer
Argue the $895 "reasonable and customary" amount is too low for emergency ALS transport.
- Request an **out-of-network exception / emergency reprocessing** — you had no choice of ambulance in an emergency.
- Ask them to pay at the in-network rate. Many plans will when the service was involuntary and emergent.

## 4. Dispute the charge directly with the ambulance company
- Request an **itemized bill** — the math looks inflated (mileage is only $360, so the base rate is ~$3,246).
- Ask whether they accept the insurer's allowed amount as payment in full (some municipal services do).

## 5. Negotiate the balance down
If you do end up owing something:
- Offer a **lump-sum settlement** (often 30–50% gets accepted).
- Ask about **financial hardship / charity care** — public fire-district services frequently have it.
- Request an **interest-free payment plan** as a fallback.

## 6. Last resort
File a complaint with your **state insurance department** and/or **state attorney general** if the billing violates state law.
