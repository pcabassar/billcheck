## The big thing: this is a nonprofit hospital

Your summary says Grace Memorial is a **nonprofit**. That matters a lot. Nonprofit hospitals are required by federal law (Section 501(r) of the IRS code) to:

- Have a written **Financial Assistance Policy (FAP)**
- **Tell patients about it** and make a reasonable effort to determine if you qualify *before* taking "extraordinary collection actions" — and **suing you and garnishing your wages are exactly the actions that count**

At ~$12,000/year income, you almost certainly would have qualified for free or heavily discounted care in 2013. If they never offered it and went straight to suing you, that's a potential violation of their legal obligations.

## What this could mean for you

- Many hospital FAPs say there's **no deadline** to apply, or allow looking back. Some have reopened old accounts, refunded garnished wages, and dropped balances when a patient applies and qualifies retroactively.
- Even setting the law aside, a hospital that realizes they skipped this step often wants to fix it quietly rather than have it scrutinized.

## A few things I can't tell yet — and they shape your options

1. **What state are you in?** Garnishment rules, interest caps, and protections for low-income people vary a lot by state. Some states protect a much larger share of low wages than federal law.
2. **Federal garnishment limit:** Generally they can take no more than 25% of disposable income, *or* the amount above 30x the federal minimum wage per week — whichever is less. At $12,000/year, you may be at or near the floor where **little or nothing should be garnishable**. It's worth checking whether they're taking too much.
