# Observation — s05-c2: Birthday rule flips primary/secondary on a NICU newborn, $270,951 in dispute

**Case:** [05-medicaid-dual-cob.md](../initial-research/cases/05-medicaid-dual-cob.md) §5.2 — two commercial plans, birthday rule retroactively flips primary; mom's plan claws back, secondary never billed; mom's plan is self-funded/ERISA.
**Input:** "Two plans… my Blue plan paid as primary then reversed everything citing the 'birthday rule'… hospital is billing us the whole NICU stay." + billing-summary PDF ($270,951 total / $207,455 NICU; Blue KC clawed back; CommunityCare OK no claim on file; Blue KC self-funded).
**Transcript:** [_raw/s05-c2-cob-birthday-rule-nicu.md](_raw/s05-c2-cob-birthday-rule-nicu.md)

## What happened
- Led with **"you almost certainly do not owe this — it's a coordination problem, not a $271k debt."**
- Explained the **birthday rule** accurately (earlier month/day = primary; year/age irrelevant) and the key insight that it changes *order*, not *coverage*.
- Diagnosed the exact two-step failure from the doc: primary clawed back **and** secondary never billed → whole balance dumped on the family. Laid out the correct sequence (CommunityCare primary → Blue KC secondary cleans up).
- Flagged **self-funded = ERISA, not state DOI** unprompted, and the **timely-filing clock** on the unfiled claim.
- Turn 2: given "husband March, me August," correctly concluded the rule was applied right (so don't fight the reversal), gave an **ordered call list (hospital → CommunityCare → Blue KC → HR)**, and surfaced the real landmine: **was the newborn actually enrolled on the husband's plan within the 30–60 day window?** — the one thing that could create genuine liability.
