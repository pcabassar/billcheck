### billcheck
You're not stuck, and your instinct is right. There's a specific federal protection that applies here, and "complications" is not an automatic excuse to nearly double the bill.

Here's what matters in your situation:

**You got a Good Faith Estimate (GFE) because you're uninsured.** Under the federal No Surprises Act, uninsured/self-pay patients are entitled to a GFE before scheduled care — which you got ($50,560). When the final bill comes in **$400 or more above** the estimate, you have the right to dispute it through a formal **Patient-Provider Dispute Resolution (PPDR)** process.

Your bill is **$97,998 billed** vs. a **$50,560 estimate** — that's roughly **$47,000 over**, far beyond the $400 threshold. This is squarely the kind of gap the dispute process exists for.

A few important points:
- "Complications cost more" can be a legitimate reason for *some* increase, but the provider has to actually justify it line by line. They can't just say it verbally and move on.
- The dispute is decided by an independent third party, not the hospital.
- There's a **deadline**: you generally must start the PPDR dispute within **120 days of the date of the bill.**

Two quick things before I help you act:

1. **What is the date on the final bill?** (Not the service date — the date the bill was issued/sent.) That sets your 120-day clock.
2. Do you want me to also pursue a **direct negotiation/itemized-records request** with the hospital in parallel? That's often faster and can resolve it without the formal dispute.

### billcheck
There's a real process — calling again won't get you anywhere because the person on the phone can just say "complications" again. Here's the actual sequence:

**Step 1 — Request the records in writing.** Ask the hospital for two things:
- A fully **itemized bill** (every line, code, and charge — not the summary you have)
- A **written explanation of why the charges exceeded the Good Faith Estimate**

Doing this in writing matters: it creates a paper trail, forces them to justify the overage on the record, and it's the foundation for everything after.

**Step 2 — Review what they send.** Often the overage isn't all "complications" — it's duplicate charges, inflated implant markups, or things that should've been in the original estimate. This is where the real leverage shows up.

**Step 3 — If they can't justify it, file the formal dispute (PPDR)** with the federal government. An independent reviewer decides — not the hospital. You must file within **120 days of the bill date**, and the filing fee is only **$25** (waived if you can't afford it).

So the honest answer: **don't just call.** The first real move is the written request in Step 1.
