### billcheck
I'm sorry you're dealing with this on top of recovering from a crash. Let me give you the honest picture, because the answer depends on one key detail.

**What's happening on this bill:** Your ambulance was out-of-network. The company charged $3,606, your insurer decided only $895 was "reasonable and customary," paid that, and the ambulance company is billing you the $2,711 difference. This is called **balance billing**, and it's exactly why your siblings paid so much less — their ambulances were probably in-network (or treated as such), so they couldn't be balance-billed.

**Is it allowed? This is the important part:** The federal No Surprises Act protects people from surprise balance bills in many emergency situations — **but it specifically does NOT cover ground ambulances.** That's a real gap in the law. So whether you can fight this depends almost entirely on your **state**, because a number of states have their own laws that DO protect against ground-ambulance balance billing.

So the one thing that changes everything here: **What state were you in when this happened?**

That tells me whether this bill is illegal (in which case you may owe only your normal in-network cost-share) or whether it's legal-but-negotiable (in which case we go after the amount instead).

A couple of other things worth knowing while you tell me the state:
- A car accident often means **auto insurance / med-pay** may cover this — sometimes before your health insurance even comes in. Do you have auto coverage that could apply?
- Valley Fire District is a **public/municipal ambulance service**, which sometimes have their own rules or hardship policies.

Want me to record the basics of your case so we can build from here? Just give me the state and whether any auto insurance is in the picture, and I'll tell you exactly which path applies.

**1. The "this bill may be illegal" track (strongest if it applies)**
If your state has a ground-ambulance balance-billing protection law, this $2,711 may not be yours to pay at all — you'd owe only your normal in-network cost-share. This is the home run, but it only works in certain states. *Need your state to confirm.*

**2. The auto-insurance track**
A car crash usually means **med-pay or PIP coverage** on your (or another driver's) auto policy. That can pay ambulance/ER bills regardless of network status — sometimes in full. This is often the fastest win and people forget it exists. *Need to know if any auto coverage is in play.*

**3. The "negotiate the amount down" track (works almost anywhere)**
Even if the bill is technically legal, $3,606 for a 12-mile ALS transport is high, and the $895 your insurer allowed shows what a "fair" rate looks like. We can:
- Ask Valley Fire District for an **itemized bill** and challenge the per-mile and base charges.
- Request a **prompt-pay or cash discount**.
- Apply for **financial hardship/charity care** — public ambulance districts frequently have this.
- Negotiate a **lump-sum settlement** (offering, say, a portion of the balance to close it out).

**4. The insurance-appeal track**
Push your health plan to reprocess the ambulance at the in-network rate because it was an **emergency you couldn't choose the provider for** — many plans will, and that shrinks your balance directly.

We'd usually run #1 and #2 first (they can eliminate the bill), then fall back to #3 and #4.
