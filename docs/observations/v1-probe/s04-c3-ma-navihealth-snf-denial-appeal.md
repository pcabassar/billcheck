Here's what's actually going on, in plain terms:

**This is a NOMNC (Notice of Medicare Non-Coverage).** It is *not* a final decision. It's the plan saying naviHealth (the company UHC hires to review stays) thinks your husband no longer needs skilled-level care. You have the right to a **Fast Appeal** through an independent reviewer called the QIO (Quality Improvement Organization). The QIO is not UnitedHealthcare — they're a separate body that decides whether the cutoff is correct.

**Why this matters for you:** If you call the QIO to appeal **by noon the day before coverage ends** (so by noon on 03/25/2026), then:
- His coverage **continues during the appeal**, and
- If you appeal in time and lose, you generally are **not** liable for the cost of care during the QIO review period.

The fact that his own doctor *and* physical therapist both say he needs ~3 more weeks and that he still can't walk or dress himself is exactly the kind of evidence that wins these appeals. "Goals substantially met" is clearly contradicted by his actual condition.

**What you need to do right away:**
1. **Call the QIO number on the notice before noon the day before the end date.** That single phone call starts the appeal and protects you financially. Do not miss this — it's the most important step.
2. When you call, say clearly: *"I am requesting a fast appeal of the discharge decision."* Give his name and member info from the notice.
3. The QIO will ask the facility to send his records and will usually decide within about 48 hours.

They'll request the facility's records, but a direct statement from his neurologist — who knows his case and disagrees — is powerful supporting evidence. Ask the QIO when you call how they want it sent (usually fax or a portal); the facility's case manager can often transmit it for you too.

**What makes her note effective.** Ask her to be specific and tie it to *skilled-level* need. The most useful statement would say:
- That he still requires **skilled nursing and/or skilled therapy** services (not just custodial care),
- His **specific current deficits** — can't walk independently, can't dress himself, etc.,
- That he is **still making progress** and has not "plateaued," and
- Her **medical opinion that discharge now is premature** and he needs roughly 3 more weeks.

That language matters because the denial reason is "goals substantially met" and "no longer meets skilled criteria." Her note should directly contradict both.

You can submit her written statement during the review. Don't let waiting on the letter cause you to miss the call.
