### billcheck
Take a breath — your own paperwork has the answer, and it's strongly in your favor.

**Short answer: No, you almost certainly do not have to pay this $3,000.**

Here's what the Medicare Summary Notice tells us:

1. **"Maximum you may be billed: $0.00."** This is the key line. Medicare is telling you, in writing, that you owe nothing.

2. **The provider accepted assignment.** That means North Atlantic Anesthesia agreed to follow Medicare's rules and accept what Medicare allows as payment in full.

3. **Denial reason CO-29 — "time limit for filing expired."** The claim wasn't filed until 03/12/2026, for a service on 10/03/2024 — well past Medicare's 12-month filing deadline. This is **the provider's mistake, not yours.**

Under Medicare rules, when a participating provider files too late and gets a CO-29 denial, **they cannot bill the patient for that amount.** The loss is theirs for missing the deadline. So the collection company is pursuing money that, by Medicare's own rules, you don't owe.

This is what's called a **provider write-off**, and the collections demand is improper.

Here's what I'd suggest we do:

1. **Send a written dispute** to the collection agency. Under the Fair Debt Collection Practices Act, sending this within 30 days of their first notice forces them to verify the debt and pause collection. The letter should cite the MSN, the CO-29 denial, the assignment, and the $0 "maximum you may be billed."

2. **Track the 30-day window** so we don't miss it.

### billcheck
Good instinct. The cardinal rule on a collections call: **never say "I'll pay" or "I owe" anything** — even a small "good faith" payment can reset the clock and be treated as you accepting the debt. Stay calm, factual, and brief.

Here's the spine of what to say:

**1. Identify and request validation (don't confirm details):**
> "I'm calling about the account you sent a notice on. I'm disputing this debt in full. Please send me written validation of the debt."

**2. State the core fact — let the paperwork do the talking:**
> "Medicare denied this claim under reason code CO-29 because the provider filed it too late. The provider accepted assignment, and the Medicare Summary Notice states the maximum I may be billed is zero dollars. Under Medicare rules, this amount is the provider's write-off and cannot be billed to the patient."

**3. Put them on notice in writing:**
> "I'm also sending this dispute in writing. Please note the account as disputed and do not report it to the credit bureaus while it's disputed."

**4. If they push back or pressure you:**
> "I'm not going to discuss payment. I've stated my dispute. Please direct any further contact to my written correspondence."

Then end the call politely.

**A few guardrails:**
- **Don't agree to a "settlement" or "partial payment."** There's nothing valid to settle.
- **Take notes:** date, time, the rep's name, and what was said.
- **Don't give new personal/financial info** beyond confirming the account in question.

Honestly, I'd lean on the **written dispute over the phone call** — a phone call leaves no proof, and the written letter is what triggers your legal protections under the FDCPA. The call is fine as a first touch, but the letter is what protects you.
