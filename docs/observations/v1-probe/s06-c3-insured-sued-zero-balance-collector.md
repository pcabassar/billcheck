I can see why this is confusing — and I want to be clear about the most urgent thing first.

**This is a real lawsuit with a hard deadline.** The summons says you have **21 days from the date you were served** to file a written response with the court. If you miss that, the company can get a **default judgment** against you — meaning they win automatically, even if the debt is bogus, and can then garnish wages or your bank account. So regardless of how wrong this feels, the response window is the thing that protects you.

A few things stand out in your favor:

- **"Credit Service Company (assignee)"** means they're a debt buyer/collector who claims the hospital handed off your account. That's common.
- **"Original creditor: (not stated)"** — they didn't even name the hospital. In court, the plaintiff has to *prove* they own the debt and that the amount is owed. A bare complaint with no documentation is weak.
- **Your hospital portal shows $0 and your insurer says it was paid.** If that's accurate, the debt may have been sent to collections in error, or before insurance posted — and you have evidence to fight it.

So you likely have a strong defense, but **you still have to show up / file on time** to use it.

1. **What date were you served** the papers (handed to you, or left/mailed)? That sets your 21-day clock.

### User
So do I just call them and explain it's already paid, or do I actually have to file something with the court? I don't want to mess this up.

### billcheck
Great question — and this is exactly where people accidentally hurt themselves.

**You have to file something with the court. Calling them is not enough — and calling alone can actually backfire.** Here's why:

- The **only thing that stops a default judgment is a written response filed with the court** within your 21 days. A phone call to the collector does *nothing* for that deadline. You could have the friendliest call in the world, miss the filing date, and still lose by default.
- Talking to a collector before you've filed can also lead you to accidentally say something that's treated as acknowledging the debt, or get pressured into a "payment plan" on a bill you don't even owe.

**The right order is:**
1. **File your written answer with the court** (this preserves all your rights and forces *them* to prove the debt).
2. In that answer, you deny the claim and raise your defenses — e.g., that insurance paid it, that they haven't shown they own the debt, that they didn't name the original creditor.
3. *Separately*, you can send the collector a **debt validation request** in writing — making them produce the actual itemized bill and proof they own it. Often a collector who can't validate just drops the case.

You don't need a lawyer to file an answer in Colorado county court — these forms are made for people representing themselves. I can draft the answer for you so you just review, sign, and file it.
