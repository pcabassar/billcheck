### billcheck
You're reading it right — the No Surprises Act **does** protect patients from giant air ambulance bills. Here's exactly how it works, because the distinction matters for your situation:

**What the law protects you from:** Balance billing. When an out-of-network air ambulance transports you, the provider can only charge you your **in-network cost-sharing** (your normal deductible, copay, coinsurance). They legally **cannot** bill you for the difference between their charge and what the insurer pays. That $97,599 "patient responsibility"? Under the NSA, that figure should not be landing on you.

**The catch in your case:** The protection is built around the assumption that the insurer *processes the claim as a covered service* and pays its share, with the leftover dispute settled between Cigna and the air ambulance company (through a separate arbitration that doesn't involve you). Cigna is trying to sidestep this by denying the whole claim as "not medically necessary" — paying $0. So they're framing it as "we don't owe anything" rather than "this is an out-of-network payment dispute."

**Why you still win either way:**
- If you get the **medical necessity denial overturned** (external review), Cigna has to pay, and the NSA caps what's left to your in-network share.
- Even while that's pending, the NSA's **balance-billing ban still applies** to that air ambulance bill — so you can push back on any attempt to collect $97,599 from you directly.

So the strategy is really two fronts at once: knock down the denial *and* invoke the billing protection. They reinforce each other.
