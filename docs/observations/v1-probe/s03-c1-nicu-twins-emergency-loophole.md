**On the baby in distress at birth**

A newborn rushed to the NICU in acute distress is the clearest possible example of an **emergency medical condition**. The legal standard for emergency care (under the No Surprises Act and most plan language) is whether a *prudent layperson* would believe the absence of immediate care could seriously endanger health. A baby in distress at delivery meets that without question.

Cigna's whole denial rests on a technicality — "the admission didn't originate in the ER." But the law doesn't say emergencies only count if they come through the ER. An emergency is defined by the *medical condition*, not the *door*. This is the heart of your appeal, and it's a strong argument. I'd push to have both babies' admissions processed as emergency care.

**On whether the state can help — let's not guess**

You're right that it depends on the plan type, and it's a real distinction:

- **Self-funded employer plans** are regulated by federal law (ERISA + the No Surprises Act), not the state. State insurance departments can't directly intervene, but the federal No Surprises Act protections still apply.
- **Fully-insured plans** are regulated by your state, so your state insurance department *can* be a powerful ally.

Either way you have protections — it just changes *who* you escalate to if Cigna doesn't budge. We don't have to figure this out before we start; the appeal arguments are the same regardless.

Here's the easiest way to find out: look at your insurance card or plan documents for the phrase **"self-funded," "self-insured," or "administered by Cigna"** (administered = usually self-funded), versus **"insured by Cigna."** If you're not sure, just tell me what your card says and I'll tell you which bucket you're in.
