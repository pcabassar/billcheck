I'm sorry you're dealing with this on top of a frightening medical emergency. Here's the key thing: **this denial is almost certainly improper, and you have strong grounds to win an appeal.**

## Why you're in a strong position

Federal law (the ACA's emergency services rule) requires marketplace plans like yours to cover emergency care **without prior authorization**, and to treat out-of-network emergency care as if it were in-network. You literally cannot prior-authorize a stay when you were airlifted unconscious — and the law recognizes that.

There are two layers that protect you here:

1. **Prudent layperson standard** — A plan must cover emergency services based on whether a reasonable person would have thought it was an emergency, not on the final diagnosis or whether authorization was obtained. Sudden memory loss + loss of consciousness clearly qualifies.

2. **No Surprises Act** — For emergency care at an out-of-network facility, you generally can only be billed your in-network cost-sharing amount. That $59,000 "member may be responsible for billed charges" and the $15,312 balance bill are exactly what this law was designed to stop.

So Molina is denying for a reason that isn't legally valid for emergency care, and the hospital's balance bill may itself be prohibited.

Once you exhaust the plan's internal appeals, you can demand an **independent external review** — a reviewer who does *not* work for Molina and whose decision **Molina is legally required to obey.** This is where denials like yours frequently get overturned, because an outside reviewer applies the prudent-layperson standard instead of Molina's internal rules.
