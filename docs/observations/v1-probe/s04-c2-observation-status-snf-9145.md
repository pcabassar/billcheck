### billcheck
I'm sorry you're dealing with this — it's one of the most frustrating gaps in Medicare, and your reaction is exactly right. Let me explain what happened and what you can actually do.

## What happened

Medicare's "3-day rule" requires **3 nights as a formal inpatient** before it will cover a skilled nursing facility (SNF) stay. Here's the catch your paperwork reveals:

- Your dad's own doctor **ordered an inpatient admission on 3/04/2026.**
- The next day, the hospital's **Utilization Review** committee reclassified him to "observation" using **Condition Code 44** — a hospital-initiated downgrade.
- Because of that downgrade, his 3 nights don't count as a "qualifying inpatient stay," so Medicare denied the $9,145 SNF bill.

So you're right: he was in a hospital bed for three nights, and his physician wanted him admitted. A back-office reclassification is what cost you the coverage. This is a known, contestable problem.

## The good news — there are real avenues here

There's been a major recent development. Because of a federal court case (*Alexander v. Azar*), Medicare patients who were reclassified from inpatient to observation now have the **right to appeal that observation status itself** — something that wasn't possible before. Your dad's situation (physician ordered inpatient, then downgraded by the hospital) is squarely the kind of case this applies to.

You potentially have a few angles:
1. **Appeal the SNF denial** through Medicare's normal appeal process.
2. **Challenge the observation reclassification** under the new appeal rights.
3. **Push the hospital directly** on the Condition Code 44 downgrade — they reclassified a physician's order.

A hospital administrative committee reversed that judgment. That's exactly the kind of decision the new appeal rights let you challenge, and it's a sympathetic, documented argument.

A few things worth knowing about Condition Code 44 downgrades:
- The hospital is supposed to follow specific rules before reclassifying — including involvement of the Utilization Review committee and notice to the physician **while the patient is still in the hospital**. If those steps weren't followed properly, that's a point in your favor.
- The treating physician's original clinical judgment carries real weight in an appeal.
- It can help to ask the admitting doctor whether they agreed with the downgrade — sometimes the physician didn't, which strengthens the case a lot.

So to answer directly: **yes, it changes things.** It moves you from "we disagree with a coverage rule" to "a documented physician order was administratively overridden, and we're contesting that." That's a much stronger appeal.
