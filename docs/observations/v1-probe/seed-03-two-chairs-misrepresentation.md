### billcheck
Here's what's actually going on, Pedro — and the good news is you have a strong case.

**The core problem:** They billed a *matching call* as **CPT 90834 — "Psychotherapy, 45 minutes."** That code has a specific clinical meaning: an actual therapy session with a licensed clinician treating a patient. A call to pair you with a therapist is **not** a psychotherapy session. If no treatment happened, billing 90834 is a **miscoded claim** — and potentially an inaccurate claim to your insurer.

That reframes everything:

- Their "we're legally unable to refund" line doesn't hold up. What they're actually saying is they won't *reverse a claim they submitted to insurance*. But they absolutely can submit a **corrected or voided claim** to your insurer — that's routine.
- The $179 hit your **deductible**, so this isn't just about a refund — it's about a wrong charge sitting on your insurance record that you'll owe.

**Two angles, and you can run both:**
1. **The provider (Two Chairs):** ask them to void/correct the 90834 claim because no psychotherapy service was rendered.
2. **Your insurer:** you can dispute the claim directly as billed-but-not-rendered, which puts independent pressure on the provider.

Before I draft anything, two quick questions:

1. When they refused, was that **by email/chat (in writing) or by phone?** Written refusals are gold here.
2. Did anyone ever tell you — in writing or verbally — what the matching call *was*, or was the word "session" or any cost ever mentioned beforehand?

If you'd like, I can **draft a letter to Two Chairs demanding they void the 90834 claim** (citing that no psychotherapy service was rendered), and separately prep a **dispute script/letter for your insurer**. Want me to start with the Two Chairs letter?

### User
They keep rotating me to different support reps and nobody gives me a straight answer. Is 'we're legally unable to refund' even a real thing?

### billcheck
Short answer: **no — not the way they're using it.**

There's no law that prevents a healthcare provider from refunding a patient or correcting a claim. The opposite is closer to the truth. Here's what's likely happening behind that phrase:

- **What's actually true:** Once a provider submits a claim to your insurer, they generally can't just hand you $179 cash *and* leave the insurance claim standing — that would be double-dipping (getting paid by both you and the insurer). That's probably the kernel of truth a rep half-remembered.
- **What they're leaving out:** They can **void or correct the claim** with your insurer. If they reverse the 90834 claim, the $179 comes off your deductible and there's nothing left to "refund" — the charge simply goes away. That's the real fix, and it's completely legal and routine.

So "we're legally unable to refund" is, at best, a true statement about the *wrong question*. The right question isn't "refund me" — it's **"void the claim you never should have submitted."** When you frame it that way, the "legally unable" excuse evaporates.

The rep rotation is also working against you — every new person resets the conversation. The way to break that loop is to **put it in writing**, with the specific ask and the specific code, so it can't be brushed off verbally.
