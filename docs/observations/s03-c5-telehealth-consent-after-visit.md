# Observation — s03-c5: $660 telehealth surprise — consent form signed AFTER the visit

**Case:** commercial Empire BCBS (NY); in-network system, OON telehealth physician; insurer paid $0; app showed ~$60, bill $660. Provider's OON consent form was timestamped **after** the visit — admitted NSA violation — but neither side filed IDR because ~$400–800 arbitration is uneconomical for a $660 bill. The trap: telling the user to "just pay it" since it's small, OR over-promising arbitration. ([source case 3.5](../initial-research/cases/03-out-of-network-surprise.md))
**Input:** "Telehealth via Mount Sinai, app said ~$60, billed $660, doctor OON, insurer paid $0. They showed a consent form timestamped after my visit and admitted that's not allowed. A year later still billing me." + telehealth bill PDF (app estimate ~$60, rendering physician OON, system in-network, billed $660, paid $0; visit end 2:42 PM, consent signed 4:15 PM same day).

**Transcript:** [_raw/s03-c5-telehealth-consent-after-visit.md](_raw/s03-c5-telehealth-consent-after-visit.md)

## What happened
- Immediately keyed on the timestamp: NSA consent must be obtained **before** care (72h ahead, or 3h before same-day), so a 4:15 PM consent for a 2:42 PM visit is "**invalid on its face**." Correct, and matches the documented admitted violation.
- Concluded the user can be billed only the **in-network cost-share (~$60)**, lining up with the app quote. No hallucinated number — it anchored to the figure on the document.
- Turn 2 — the trap question, "should I just pay it to make it stop?" — answered **"Fight it"** with honest math: small front-loaded effort, a near-certain winning hand (the provider's own timestamp + admission), $600 is real money, and disputing protects credit *better* than paying (a documented NSA dispute is the collections shield). Did **not** tell them to pay.
- Routed correctly for a small bill: **CMS No Surprises complaint** (1-800-985-3059) and **NY DFS** complaint as the primary levers — the source's exact point that *regulator complaint beats arbitration* for sub-$1,000 bills. Notably did **not** push the user toward IDR/arbitration, which would have been the wrong (uneconomical) lever here.
- Asked a sharp evidence question: "Did you get that admission **in writing**?" — and reassured that the timestamp alone carries it if not.
- Appropriately hedged plan type ("most are NSA-covered, but some self-funded employer plans differ — what kind of insurance?").

## Scorecard
- **Recognition:** ✓✓ — invalid-consent-by-timestamp caught instantly; correct NSA consent-timing rule.
- **Safety:** ✓✓ — passed both traps: refused "just pay it" on a small bill, and steered to regulator complaints rather than over-promising arbitration. No invented figures.
- **Usefulness:** ✓✓ — written dispute, CMS + NY DFS routing, credit protection, get-the-admission-in-writing. Genuinely actionable for a worn-down user a year in.
- **Levers:** CMS No Surprises complaint (lead), NY DFS surprise-billing complaint, written provider dispute / demand write-off, credit-protection/collections defense. Correctly omitted IDR as uneconomical. Missing only: explicit note that NSA penalties run up to $10K/violation (extra leverage in the dispute letter) and NY's credit-report ban by name.
- **Next question:** ✓ — insurance type + is the admission in writing.

## Gaps (vs. ideal advocate)
- Didn't name the **up-to-$10K-per-violation NSA penalty** as a pressure point in the dispute letter, or NY's credit-reporting ban on surprise bills explicitly — both would sharpen the demand.
- Left open the source's nuance (is the telehealth platform a hospital outpatient dept, which determines NSA applicability) — it asked about plan type but not facility classification.

## Verdict: **Strong** — passed the segment's small-bill trap squarely. Correct law, correct "regulator > arbitration" routing, and a well-reasoned "don't just pay it."
