# Observation — s03-c1: NICU twins ~$80K — insurer says it wasn't an "emergency"

**Case:** commercial Cigna, NICU newborns billed fully OON; insurer refused "emergency" status because admission entered via obstetrics, not the ER → ~$80K, collections ([source case 3.1](../initial-research/cases/03-out-of-network-surprise.md))
**Input:** "Our twins spent weeks in the NICU… Cigna says out of network… we owe ~$80,000 and it's in collections. How is that not an emergency?" + Cigna EOB PDF (billed $117,084, plan paid $37,000, patient resp. $80,084, OON OOP max $10,000, "admission source: OBSTETRICS — not processed as emergency").

**Transcript:** [_raw/s03-c1-nicu-twins-emergency-loophole.md](_raw/s03-c1-nicu-twins-emergency-loophole.md)

## What happened
- Nailed the exact loophole: Cigna conflates "emergency" with "came through the ER," when the legal test is the **medical condition** (prudent-layperson standard), not the door. Called "did not originate in the ER" the weak point to attack.
- Spotted a second angle from the document itself — the **$10K OON OOP max** on the EOB doesn't square with an $80K balance, flagging either improper balance billing or a processing error.
- Turn 2: when the user mentioned newborn distress, correctly elevated it to "emergency medical condition" and told them to pull delivery/NICU records, APGAR scores, attending notes as ammunition.
- Handled the **self-funded vs fully-insured** question cleanly: explained the distinction, named the right regulator for each (state DOI vs DOL/ERISA), and correctly noted the NSA applies either way so they don't lose their main tool. Told them to find out via SPD or HR.
- Safe on money: "don't let the $80K stand," send a written collections dispute to protect credit, file a formal appeal. Did not tell them to pay.
